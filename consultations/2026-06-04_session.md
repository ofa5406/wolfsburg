# Mobility Vision

Wolfsburg Post\-Car Vision: Proving the Shared Mobility System Works<br>4 June 2026 at 09:30

Urban Design Studio — Project Review / Wolfsburg Future Mobility

Overview<br>The team presented *Post-Car Future of Wolfsburg*, a speculative urban design project reimagining Wolfsburg as a post\-private\-car city\. The core argument is that the city currently surrenders a disproportionate share of its land to cars — roads, parking lots, and traffic infrastructure — at the direct expense of public life\. The proposed vision replaces private car dependency with an integrated system of autonomous shared vehicles, cycling infrastructure, and a tiered network of mobility hubs\. The review session ended with strong encouragement of the vision's direction, but a clear challenge was issued: the team must now move beyond compelling images and prove the system can actually work\.

The Vision: Street Transformation and Urban Reclaim

The team opened with before\-and\-after visualisations of specific Wolfsburg streets — Kleiststrasse, Schillerstrasse, and Roten Pelagic — showing how removing on\-street parking and reducing car lanes could unlock space for wider footpaths, protected cycling lanes, trees, seating, and active ground\-floor uses\. A large canal\-side parking lot was shown transformed into a park\.

The underlying logic is straightforward: Wolfsburg was essentially built around Volkswagen's commuting peaks\. Tens of thousands of workers arrive and depart on a schedule, flooding the city with cars that then sit idle for hours\. The team's proposition is that cars should be moving assets in a shared fleet, not stationary private property\. A scheduled shift system at the factory — roughly 10,000 workers per wave — actually makes a shared autonomous fleet more viable here than in most cities, because demand is predictable\.

> *"The goal is not only to move people more efficiently but to give them the city back\."*

The Mobility System: Five Modes, Three Hub Sizes

The proposed network integrates five vehicle types: autonomous shuttles, autonomous buses, shared EV cars, autonomous pods, and e\-cargo bikes\. These are tied together through a hierarchy of mobility hubs in three sizes — L, M, and S\.

• L\-hubs repurpose existing multi\-storey car parks \(six identified in the city centre\)\. Ground floors become social/commercial space; upper floors handle fleet charging, maintenance, and storage\.<br>• M\-hubs are distributed throughout the urban fabric, combining bike\-sharing, EV charging bays, and transfer points between modes\.<br>• S\-hubs are minimal nodes — a charging point at a bus stop, a single e\-bike dock\.

The team claims this network covers approximately 97–100% of the city centre, with 78\+ hub locations identified\. They also quantified how much land currently dedicated to parking could be reclaimed — more than 10% of the city centre is surface parking alone, before counting roadside spaces\.

A useful suggestion came from the room: *show one image that communicates the full scale of car\-dedicated land in the city*\. The scattered blue\-dot map of parking areas was striking, but a single composite visualisation could be far more powerful rhetorically\.

The Critical Challenge: From Vision to Proof

This was where the session's energy concentrated\. Several reviewers acknowledged the vision's appeal but pushed hard on one question: how do you prove this works?

The comparison made repeatedly was to the development of public transit networks\. The idea that a non\-wealthy person could step outside their house and board a vehicle that takes them across the city was once considered utopian — until someone had to design the actual metro network, count the stations, calculate the headways, and define what "good enough coverage" meant\. The team is now standing in front of exactly that second task\.

> *"The vision is just the first kick\. The interesting thing is now what is needed to do the transformation — how many hubs, where, and how do you convince me this is going to work? That's the core of the project\."*

Specific directions proposed by reviewers included:

• A user journey / persona simulation — follow one Volkswagen shift worker from home to factory gate under the new system, showing travel time, mode changes, and convenience compared to driving\. Then aggregate this to district level\.<br>• A demand\-based sizing exercise — if roughly 40,000 city residents need to reach the factory across scheduled shift waves, how large does the autonomous fleet need to be? Where must hubs be placed to make walk distances acceptable?<br>• A transformation timeline — how does Wolfsburg get from here to there? Munich's U\-Bahn was built incrementally, anchored to the 1972 Olympics deadline\. The factory's daily commuter load could serve as an equivalent forcing function\.<br>• References to real precedents — cities in the Netherlands that daylighted buried canals, Barcelona's superblocks, Paris's street reclamation — to show this is not fantasy but an extension of ongoing urban practice\.

One reviewer also pushed the team to go further in imagining what reclaimed space *becomes* — not just a greener traffic corridor, but potentially spaces re\-assigned to residents as gardens, productive urban land, or genuinely different public programmes\. The transformation from car space to *people space* shouldn't default to a tidier version of the same street typology\.

Key Decisions &amp; Next Steps

• \[Core proof / whole team\]: Define a quantifiable goal — e\.g\. "80% of residents can reach the Volkswagen campus within 20 minutes without a private car" — and build the hub placement and fleet sizing logic around demonstrating it\.<br>• \[User story / whole team\]: Develop at least one detailed persona journey \(e\.g\. shift worker, city resident, elderly non\-driver\) that traces the new system end\-to\-end and makes the experience legible at human scale\.<br>• \[Visualisation / whole team\]: Produce a single map or graphic that communicates the full extent of car\-dedicated land in Wolfsburg today — this was flagged as potentially the project's most rhetorically powerful image\.<br>• \[References / whole team\]: Gather 3–5 real\-world precedents \(Netherlands canal restorations, Paris, Barcelona\) to ground the vision and pre\-empt "this is pure fantasy" criticism\.<br>• \[Reclaimed space / whole team\]: Revisit what happens to freed land beyond "nicer street" — consider more transformative, unexpected programming tied to adjacent residents or community use\.<br>• \[Strategic focus / whole team\]: Choose one genuinely difficult sub\-problem — fleet sizing, transformation sequencing, financing model — and go deep on it\. Difficulty is where the interesting design work and the credible ideas live\.
