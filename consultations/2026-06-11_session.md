# Mobility

Overview<br>This was a working review session in which a student team presented their mobility transformation concept for Wolfsburg's city centre\. The team had completed a substantial quantitative foundation — calculating trip demand, fleet sizing, and hub infrastructure requirements — and were beginning to translate that data into a spatial master plan\. The supervising critic and peers offered focused feedback on hub placement strategy, street transformation visualisation, and how to communicate the project's complexity convincingly\. The session ended with clear direction on what to design and model over the coming two weeks\.

Demand Calculations &amp; Fleet Sizing

The team established that the city centre serves roughly 17,000 residents, 18,000 workers, and 17,000 visitors, generating approximately 100,000 trips per day\. Currently the modal split is heavily car\-dominated, with public transport at 20% and cycling at only 8%\. The critical planning figure is the peak hour load of ~9,000 trips, which the team used as the basis for all fleet calculations\.

The central question was: if all private car trips were replaced by a shared mobility system, what fleet would be needed? The team's answer:

• 131 e\-bikes<br>• 55 auto\-shuttle pods<br>• 33 autobuses<br>• 369 auto\-pods<br>• 175 car\-sharing vehicles

A dot\-matrix graphic was used to show the equivalent number of private cars replaced — each dot representing 10 vehicles — which the critic singled out as an especially effective communication device\.

From the fleet, the team derived hub counts: 6 large hubs \(Hub L\), 19 medium \(Hub M\), and 43 small \(Hub S\), with approximate footprints of ~2,000 m² / 300 m² / 91 m² respectively\.

> *"This was a very good basis — from there the concept is much more grounded\."*

Traffic Plan &amp; Zone Structure

Drawing inspiration from Groningen, Netherlands, the team divided the city centre core into five zones, four of which border Porsche Strasse\. The key principle is preventing inter\-zone through\-traffic: internal streets are one\-direction only, and zone entrances are accessible only from a defined ring road\. This effectively eliminates rat\-running while keeping access possible\.

The street cross\-section strategy was also presented in tiers: removing on\-street parking and reducing to two lanes creates space for drop\-off zones \(usable now for public transport, later for autonomous vehicles\); reducing to one lane frees even more ground for pedestrians\. The critic appreciated the mechanical clarity of this logic and suggested a complementary behavioural tool — manipulating traffic light intervals to make car access unattractive without outright prohibition, gradually shifting modal behaviour\.

A question was raised about the northern arterial road, which currently cuts the city centre off from developing areas and the river\. Several options were floated — rerouting closer to the railway, opening the freed land as green space — and the critic noted that since the city likely already has plans for this strip, the project should acknowledge and complement those plans rather than propose a competing solution\.

Hub Placement &amp; Design Strategy

A key debate centred on whether large hubs belong in the centre or the periphery\. The team's instinct was to place Hub L centrally — close to the train station, on major access roads, and reusing an existing multi\-storey car park — partly because they want hubs to function as social infrastructure, not just parking\. The critic challenged this, noting that a large hub in the centre will still attract significant car movement, which may conflict with the pedestrianisation goals\.

> *"If you place a big hub next to a pedestrian zone, there will be a lot of cars going out\."*

The team clarified that their primary goal is reclaiming space occupied by parked cars, not necessarily eliminating car trips entirely — a useful distinction\. The critic suggested a pragmatic resolution: some hubs inside, some on the periphery, calibrated by function\. For Volkswagen factory workers specifically, a hub near the factory or its access tunnels was proposed as a logical and narratively compelling case study\.

On e\-bikes and scooters, the critic noted these are typically free\-floating in real deployments, redistributed by operators rather than tied to fixed hubs\. The team acknowledged the trade\-off between user preference \(free\-floating\) and the project's organisational logic \(consolidated, charged, legible hubs\)\. The consensus was that this level of operational detail is secondary to the spatial design argument, but that charging infrastructure does justify some fixed presence\.

Behavioural Change &amp; Transformation Strategy

The session opened a broader discussion about how people are motivated to give up private cars\. The critic asked the team to research real\-world strategies — incentive schemes, regulatory levers, infrastructure sequencing — and integrate a transformation timeline into the master plan\. One team member referenced an analogy from a separate presentation: just as people gave up private land ownership when moving to cities, private car ownership may diminish organically as autonomous mobility\-as\-a\-service makes ownership economically irrational — no insurance liability, no parking cost, always available\.

The critic's practical concern was political acceptance: residents who see their streets blocked without a positive vision will resist\. Visualising the "after" state — what happens to parking lots, what inner\-block courtyards become, how a street feels once lanes are removed — was identified as essential for public and stakeholder communication\.

Computational vs\. Manual Design

The team was debating how much of the hub\-placement logic to encode in Grasshopper versus resolving manually\. The critic's advice was pragmatic: the algorithm is most valuable for placing the 43 small hubs, where manual iteration is impractical\. For the 6 large hubs, manual design is appropriate and actually necessary to embed contextual judgment\. The key principle:

> *"These tools shall be helpful, but now you've defined your concept — everything should support the project, not consume it\."*

The recommendation was to establish the general distribution computationally, then manually fix edge cases, and use a hand\-modelled hub as input for visualisation rather than waiting for a fully parametric model\.

Key Decisions &amp; Next Steps

• Fleet &amp; hub calculations \[Team\]: Baseline is accepted and solid\.
