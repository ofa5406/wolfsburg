# Hub Typology Design

*Created: June 15, 2026. Developed through design session.*

This document defines the design logic, spatial system, and element palette for Wolfsburg's three hub tiers (S, M, L). It is the working brief for typology drawings, Rhino modeling, and all subsequent visualization.

---

## Design Principles

### 1. System Identity
The 68 hubs form a recognizable network across Wolfsburg — a design family with consistent language that makes the system visible, navigable, and tactilely coherent. Every hub belongs to the same system. Every hub is also responsive to its immediate context. **Modular element types, context-specific arrangement.**

### 2. Ground Field
Every hub occupies a **distinctive ground field** of reddish stone cube paving. This field:
- Defines the hub's territory and separates it visually from surrounding pavement
- Is rectangular in geometry, aligned with the street's direction and orthogonality
- Scales to the available space — dimensions are not fixed, they are read from the street section and context
- Contains directional lines embedded in the surface, navigating users toward mobility elements
- Integrates tactile paving strips at all transitions, boarding zones, and crossings for disability accessibility

### 3. Vertical Beacon
Every hub has a **canopy or structural element** readable from 50 metres. This is the hub's vertical signal in the city — its presence from a distance. The canopy may combine with a public transport shed into a single unified structure where a bus route overlaps.

### 4. Smooth Usage
The spatial arrangement guides users without additional signage. Directional lines in the stone field, element positions, and the three-zone logic create a legible sequence that works intuitively in both directions of use.

### 5. Catalyst, Not Shelter
Hubs are not transit shelters. They are new anchors of public life — environmentally generous (trees, planting, ecological infrastructure), socially generous (seating, shelter, social elements), and spatially generous (quality public space, programme). Mobility is the anchor; social and cultural life is the ambition.

---

## Street Connectivity

A hub connects both sides of the street. Three strategies apply depending on street width and traffic type — they can combine, and different strategies can apply to different faces of the same corner hub:

1. **Stone field crosses the carriageway** — reddish paving continues across the street; directional lines guide the crossing; the hub reads as one piece spanning both sides
2. **Raised / narrowed carriageway** — the hub creates a traffic calming moment; the raised or narrowed surface slows vehicles and integrates the crossing into the hub
3. **Elements on both sides** — the hub distributes its elements across the street, with the crossing as the link between two halves

---

## Three-Zone Spatial Logic

Elements arrange across the ground field in three zones running from the carriageway inward. This is a **design principle, not a rigid template** — real contexts compress, expand, or reorder zones as needed.

Corner hubs wrap: Zone 1 faces both street directions, Zone 3 occupies the inner corner.

```
[Carriageway]
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
ZONE 1 — VEHICLE EDGE
Docking, arrival/departure, EV bay, charging
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
ZONE 2 — THRESHOLD
Information terminal, identity marker, wayfinding
Canopy overhead
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
ZONE 3 — DWELLING EDGE
Seating, shelter, greenery, social/cultural elements
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
[Building edge / pavement / open space]
```

The sequence works in both directions: arriving by vehicle or arriving on foot, the arrangement remains legible.

|---------|-----------|

| Cargo bike dock | Docking for cargo bikes (shared or private). Zone 1. |

| Hub identity marker | Panel or integrated element: hub name, tier level (S/M/L), map of nearby hubs. |

| Bike repair station | Self-service tools and pump. Integrated at Zone 3 edge. |

| Element | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| Tree with stone grate | Tree planted within the stone field; grate integrated flush with paving. Shade, identity, climate benefit. |
| Planting bed / bioswale strip | Linear planted bed, may serve as stormwater channel. At Zone 3 edge or between zones. |
| Permeable surface zones | Sections of ground field using permeable paving for stormwater infiltration. |

| Element | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| Community information board | Physical noticeboard for local events and neighbourhood information. Zone 3. |
| Vendor / kiosk slot | Defined position for a small vendor (coffee, local goods). M and L. |
| Art or cultural marker | Site-specific artwork integrated into the hub — one per hub, designed with the site. |
| Flexible surface area | Clear, unobstructed paved area for temporary events, markets, gatherings. M and L. |

### Lighting

| Element | Definition |
|---------|-----------|

## Tier Definitions

### S-HUB — The Moment
*43 hubs · Last-mile · E-bike + micro-pod · Walking radius: 150–250 m*

The S-hub is a moment in the street. You arrive, grab a bike, or wait briefly for a micro-pod, and you leave. The design is compact but unmistakably a hub — never a bike rack on pavement.

**Mandatory:**
E-bike docking rack, private bike parking, micro-pod arrival/departure zone, charging points, hub identity marker, directional lines in stone surface, tactile paving strips, canopy/structural element, minimum 1 bench, ground-embedded lighting

**Standard** *(present at most S-hubs):*
Tree with stone grate, ambient responsive lighting, community information board, art or cultural marker, PT shed combined with canopy (where bus route overlaps), hub-integrated crossing (where street context requires)

---

### M-HUB — The Choice Point
*19 hubs · Neighbourhood scale · Multimodal transfer · Walking radius: 300–500 m*

The M-hub is where you have options. Multiple modes are available, there may be a short wait, and you are likely transferring. The design is more generous: a real place, not a pause. At an M-hub you can see what vehicles are available — live — before you decide.

**Mandatory** *(everything at S, plus):*
Cargo bike dock, real-time information terminal with live vehicle availability display, shared EV bay, autonomous vehicle drop-off/pickup zone, larger shelter/canopy (or combined with PT shed), bench + group seating, drinking water point, bike repair station, planting bed/bioswale strip, permeable surface zones, tree with stone grate (minimum one)

**Standard:**
PT shed (where route overlaps), vendor/kiosk slot, flexible surface area, raised/narrowed carriageway at crossing, element distribution across both sides of street

---

### L-HUB — The Neighbourhood Anchor
*6 hubs · Repurposed car parks or pavilion clusters · Full fleet · Walking radius: 700–1000 m*

The L-hub is a neighbourhood anchor — a different category, not a scaled-up M. The mobility infrastructure is the base; a community programme defines the character. The building or ensemble IS the identity. No canopy is needed as a beacon.

**Mobility infrastructure (mandatory):**
Autonomous vehicle staging area, EV fleet charging at scale, fleet maintenance station, full fleet representation (all five modes)

**Two scenarios:**

**Scenario A — Building reuse** *(primary case: repurposed multi-storey car parks)*
Ground floor: gastronomy (café, restaurant), shops, cultural and community spaces. Not vendors — permanent or semi-permanent programme. Upper floors open for new use (housing, studios, event space). The existing building structure is retained; the ground floor is transformed. Mobility infrastructure operates at the ground floor perimeter and in the surrounding open space.

**Scenario B — No existing building**
Pavilions arranged around a generous open space. Each pavilion carries a distinct programme (gastronomy, cultural, community). Mobility infrastructure at the perimeter. The open space is the social heart of the L-hub.

**Outdoor space (both scenarios):**
All S and M outdoor elements apply. Additionally: game tables, generous planting and tree canopy, living wall on building facade or pavilion walls, memory/history marker, pop-up market slot, flexible outdoor surface for events and markets.

## Hub Ground Field Dimensions

Hub ground fields include road surface — they are **street section redesigns**, not sidewalk installations. The reddish stone field spans the carriageway. Dimensions are approximate and context-responsive; the values below are working ranges, not fixed templates.

| Tier | Width (across street) | Length (along street) | Typical street context |
|------|----------------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| S | 10–15 m | 15–20 m | Residential / secondary street (5–8m carriageway + pavement both sides) |
| M | 18–25 m | 25–35 m | Main neighbourhood street (8–12m carriageway) |
| L | 30–50 m (outdoor area) | 40–60 m | Major street / square / campus edge |

**Visibility principle:** Element spacing uses the full available area. Elements must be visible and legible from a comfortable approach distance — hubs are not compressed to minimum functional footprint.

**How the dimensions relate to street sections:**
- At an S-hub, the stone field reclaims the pavement on both sides plus the full carriageway width at that point — the street becomes a shared surface for the length of the hub.
- At an M-hub, the hub may occupy a full lane or former parking strip on one or both sides in addition to the carriageway crossing.
- L-hubs are defined by the building or pavilion cluster plus a generous outdoor mobility and social zone surrounding them.
