# Wolfsburg Post-Car Vision — Project Description and Continuation Brief

**Project title:** *Post-Car Future of Wolfsburg*  
**Studio context:** Urban Design Studio — Wolfsburg Future Mobility  
**Review date:** 4 June 2026  
**Core theme:** Post-private-car urban transformation through a hub-based multimodal shared mobility system.

---

## 1. One-Sentence Project Thesis

This project reimagines Wolfsburg as a post-private-car city where the organizing element of urban mobility shifts from **parking** to **mobility hubs**, enabling autonomous shared mobility, MaaS, cycling, public transport, and redesigned streets to reclaim car-dominated land for public life.

---

## 2. Core Argument

Wolfsburg currently gives a disproportionate amount of urban land to private cars: roads, surface parking, on-street parking, traffic infrastructure, and car-oriented street geometry. This is especially visible because the city’s urban form is strongly shaped by Volkswagen commuting patterns. Tens of thousands of workers arrive and leave according to predictable shift waves, and large parts of the city are organized around the storage and movement of private vehicles.

The project argues that this land-use model is no longer necessary if mobility is reorganized around a shared, multimodal, autonomous system. Private cars currently require parking everywhere because the city is structured around individual vehicle ownership. In the proposed future, shared mobility fleets are moving assets rather than stationary private property. Therefore, land currently used for parking and traffic storage can be reclaimed for public space, greenery, active ground floors, social infrastructure, cycling, and new forms of urban life.

The aim is not simply to add autonomous vehicles to the existing city. The aim is to redesign the relationship between mobility, land, ownership, and public space.

---

## 3. Central Concept: From Parking City to Hub City

A key conceptual breakthrough is:

> In traditional car-based cities, the organizing element is **parking**.  
> In the Wolfsburg post-car vision, the organizing element becomes the **mobility hub**.

This distinction is central to the whole project.

In the current city, a typical journey is structured by parking:

1. Walk to a privately owned parked car.
2. Drive through the street network.
3. Search for parking near the destination.
4. Walk from the parking space to the destination.

This makes parking lots, garages, curbside spaces, driveways, and access roads the hidden infrastructure of everyday life. Parking is not just a technical necessity; it is the spatial system that makes private car ownership possible.

In the proposed city, a typical journey is structured by hubs:

1. Walk to the nearest mobility hub.
2. Choose or book the appropriate mode.
3. Travel via shared autonomous vehicle, bus, shuttle, bike, pod, or other mode.
4. Transfer if needed at another hub.
5. Arrive near the destination and complete the final short walk.

The mobility hub takes over many of the functions that parking currently performs, but with a different urban logic. Parking stores inactive private vehicles. Hubs organize shared access, charging, transfers, fleet circulation, maintenance, and multimodal choice.

This is why hubs should be treated as the most important physical layer of the project.

## 4. Why “The Hubs Are Your Stations”

Reviewers asking “How do we know this system works?” are partly asking a transport-planning question. If this project were a metro proposal, they would immediately ask:

- Where are the stations?
- How far do people walk to them?
- How many people does each station serve?
- How often do vehicles arrive?
- How many passengers can the system handle?
- What happens at peak times?

For a rail network, the train itself is not the main proof of the system. The station network is. The location, spacing, hierarchy, and capacity of stations determine whether the system is usable.

In this project, mobility hubs play a similar role.

They are not exactly railway stations, but they are the fixed spatial structure that makes the shared mobility system legible and operational. Autonomous vehicles can change over time. Apps can change. Vehicle types can change. But the hub network is the spatial skeleton of the future mobility system.

Therefore, the project should spend more attention on hubs than on individual vehicle renderings. The vehicles are visually futuristic, but the hubs prove the system.

The key review question becomes:

> Are the hubs located, sized, and connected in a way that makes private car ownership unnecessary for everyday life?

## 5. System Layers

The project is not only about autonomous vehicles. It is a combined mobility and urban design system made of several interdependent layers.

### 5.1 Autonomous Vehicles

Autonomous vehicles are the enabling technology. They make shared mobility more plausible because they reduce dependence on individual drivers and can allow high fleet utilization, flexible routing, 24-hour service, predictable dispatching, and lower operating constraints.

However, AVs alone do not create a post-car city. Without network design, policy, hubs, MaaS, and urban transformation, autonomous vehicles may simply reproduce car dependency in a new technological form.

The project should therefore present AVs not as the whole solution, but as one enabling component of a larger urban system.

### 5.2 Mobility as a Service (MaaS)

MaaS is the ownership and access model. Instead of one person owning one vehicle, many users access a shared fleet through a service layer. This allows mobility to shift from possession to access.

The project’s deeper argument is that autonomous vehicles make MaaS more psychologically and practically plausible. When the individual no longer operates the vehicle, the emotional and legal logic of ownership weakens. Liability, maintenance, navigation, charging, and operational responsibility can shift toward fleet operators, municipalities, or mobility providers.

This changes the vehicle from a privately controlled object into something closer to urban infrastructure: accessed when needed, but not personally owned.

### 5.3 Mobility Hubs

Mobility hubs are the most important physical layer. They are the places where the system touches the city and the user.

They serve as:

- Pick-up and drop-off points.
- Transfer points between modes.
- Charging infrastructure.
- Storage and redistribution points.
- Fleet maintenance and management locations.
- Public information points.
- Digital-physical interfaces.
- Urban anchors for social and commercial activity.

The hub hierarchy includes:

#### L-Hubs

Large mobility hubs, potentially created by repurposing existing multi-storey car parks. Six existing central multi-storey car parks were identified as possible L-hub candidates. Their ground floors can become public, social, or commercial spaces, while upper floors can support fleet charging, maintenance, storage, logistics, and shared vehicle management.

L-hubs are the backbone of the network.

#### M-Hubs

Medium hubs distributed throughout the urban fabric. These combine shared bikes, EV charging, shuttle stops, transfer points, parcel/logistics functions, and local mobility services.

M-hubs create neighborhood-level access.

#### S-Hubs

Small, minimal mobility nodes. These may be a charging point at a bus stop, a small e-bike dock, an autonomous pod stop, or a micro-transfer point.

S-hubs create fine-grain coverage and last-mile access.

### 5.4 Network and Traffic Design

A shared mobility system only works if the street network supports it. The project should consider how routes, access, traffic hierarchy, and modal priority are redesigned.

Key questions:

- Which trips should remain direct?
- Which car movements should be discouraged?
- Which streets prioritize cycling, walking, shuttles, or buses?
- How does the network handle Volkswagen shift waves?
- How are hubs connected to each other?
- What is the acceptable walking distance to a hub?
- How are transfers minimized or made comfortable?
- How does the system reduce unnecessary through-traffic?

The project can learn from historic traffic circulation plans and filtered permeability strategies. The lesson is not to replace the AV vision with cycling or conventional traffic calming, but to recognize that behavior changes when the network changes. People shift modes when alternatives become more direct, reliable, and convenient than private driving.

### 5.5 Urban Design

Urban design is the ultimate reason for the mobility transformation.

The goal is not only to move people efficiently. The goal is to give the city back to people.

Freed car space can become:

- Wider sidewalks.
- Protected cycling lanes.
- Trees and climate infrastructure.
- Public seating.
- Pocket parks.
- Urban gardens.
- Play spaces.
- Productive landscapes.
- Outdoor workspaces.
- Social infrastructure.
- Active ground-floor uses.
- New residential or community programs.
- Canal-side public space.
- Event spaces and flexible civic surfaces.

The project should avoid showing reclaimed streets only as “nicer traffic corridors.” The more interesting challenge is to ask what fundamentally new types of urban life become possible when parking and car storage are removed.

### 5.6 Digital Interface

The digital interface is the invisible layer that makes the system understandable.

In a private car city, the user already knows the system: where their car is, how to drive it, where to park, and how to navigate roads. In a shared multimodal system, the interface must replace that certainty.

The app or interface must show:

- Available modes.
- Vehicle arrival times.
- Hub locations.
- Transfer options.
- Pricing.
- Route comparison.
- Walking distance.
- Service reliability.
- Accessibility options.
- Bike or cargo-bike availability.
- Real-time disruptions.
- Personalized mobility subscriptions.

The digital interface is not just a booking tool. It is the cognitive map of the post-car city.

## 6. Why People Might Shift From Private Car Ownership to MaaS

A key theoretical argument of the project is that autonomous mobility can change the psychology of vehicle ownership.

As humanity moved from rural to urban life, the meaning of ownership shifted. Rural life was built around direct private control: land, property, tools, animals, and personal resources. Urban life made people dependent on shared infrastructure: streets, utilities, public transport, water, sewage, electricity, and communication networks. Nobody individually owns these systems, but everyone depends on them.

The private car remains one of the last strongholds of private ownership within urban life. This is not only because cars are convenient, but because the driver is personally responsible for operation. The owner is also the operator, controller, decision-maker, and risk-bearer. This creates a strong psychological foundation for ownership: control, identity, safety, responsibility, and autonomy.

Autonomous vehicles break this relationship.

If the vehicle drives itself, the user is no longer the operator in the traditional sense. Responsibility for safety, navigation, maintenance, charging, liability, and fleet management shifts away from the individual toward firms, municipalities, or public mobility operators.

At that point, using a vehicle becomes psychologically closer to using an elevator, train, tram, or public utility. The user does not need to own the machine because they are no longer responsible for operating it.

This makes MaaS more plausible. People do not shift to MaaS simply because sharing is cheaper or more sustainable. They shift because autonomous mobility weakens the functional and emotional reasons for owning a car in the first place.

In this framing:

> AVs do not merely change transport technology. They change the ownership logic of mobility.

## 7. Wolfsburg as a Special Case

Wolfsburg is unusually suited to this exploration because its mobility demand is strongly structured by Volkswagen.

The city was largely shaped around industrial commuting. Large numbers of workers move toward the factory campus at predictable times and leave in scheduled waves. Instead of treating this as a problem, the project treats it as an opportunity.

A city with predictable commuter demand is more favorable for shared autonomous mobility than a city with completely dispersed and unpredictable trips.

The project can argue:

- Volkswagen shift waves create predictable peak demand.
- Predictable demand makes fleet sizing more feasible.
- Shared vehicles can be scheduled and distributed around known commuting patterns.
- Large existing parking structures can be repurposed as L-hubs.
- Surface parking and roadside parking can be reclaimed.
- The city centre can become less dependent on private car storage.
- The factory commute can become the testing ground for the wider urban mobility transition.

This gives Wolfsburg a strong reason to become a prototype post-private-car city.

## 11. Suggested Quantifiable Goal

A useful next step is to define one measurable goal around which the system can be tested.

Possible goal:

> 80% of Wolfsburg city-centre residents can reach the Volkswagen campus within 20 minutes without using a private car.

Alternative goals:

> 90% of residents are within 300 meters of an S- or M-hub.

> 80% of Volkswagen shift workers can reach the factory gate within 25 minutes using the shared mobility network.

> The system can absorb one 10,000-worker shift wave with a defined fleet size and acceptable wait time.

> 50% of surface parking in the city centre can be removed without reducing accessibility.

The project should choose one main metric and build the proof around it.

## 12. Proof Framework

To convince reviewers, the project should develop a simple but rigorous proof framework.

### 12.1 Hub Coverage

Show:

- All L, M, and S hub locations.
- Walking radii from each hub.
- City-centre coverage.
- Residential coverage.
- Volkswagen campus access.
- Gaps in the network.
- Why each hub type is located where it is.

Possible walking-distance assumptions:

- S-hub: 150–250 m.
- M-hub: 300–500 m.
- L-hub: 700–1000 m catchment, depending on role.
- Bike access can extend catchments further.

The exact numbers can be adjusted, but assumptions must be clearly stated.

### 12.2 Fleet Sizing

Develop a simplified fleet-sizing calculation for Volkswagen shift waves.

Inputs:

- Number of workers per shift wave.
- Average vehicle capacity.
- Average trip length.
- Round-trip time.
- Loading/unloading time.
- Desired maximum wait time.
- Share of trips by mode.
- Number of workers who cycle, walk, use bus, shuttle, pod, or shared EV.

Outputs:

- Number of autonomous buses.
- Number of autonomous shuttles.
- Number of pods.
- Number of shared EVs.
- Number of e-bikes and cargo bikes.
- Required charging/storage capacity at L- and M-hubs.

The calculation does not need to be perfect, but it must show logic.

### 12.3 Persona Journey

Develop at least one detailed journey.

Example persona:

**Name:** Anna  
**Role:** Volkswagen shift worker  
**Home:** Residential neighborhood east of city centre  
**Destination:** Volkswagen factory gate  
**Trip today:** Walk to parked car → drive → traffic congestion → park → walk to gate  
**Trip in proposed system:** Walk 3 minutes to S-hub → autonomous shuttle to M-hub → direct AV bus to factory L-hub → walk 4 minutes to gate

Show:

- Total travel time.
- Walking time.
- Waiting time.
- Transfer time.
- Cost or subscription type.
- Comfort and reliability.
- Comparison with private car.
- What happens during shift peaks.

Additional personas:

- Elderly non-driver.
- Parent with child.
- City-centre resident.
- Visitor arriving by train.
- Shop owner or delivery operator.
- Student or young worker.
- Resident using cargo bike for shopping.

### 12.4 Transformation Timeline

Show how Wolfsburg moves from today to the future.

Possible phases:

#### Phase 1: Pilot and Visibility

- Identify one corridor or district.
- Introduce temporary street transformations.
- Add S- and M-hubs.
- Pilot shared e-bikes, shuttles, and digital MaaS interface.
- Use tactical urbanism and reversible changes.

#### Phase 2: Hub Backbone

- Convert existing multi-storey car parks into L-hubs.
- Add charging, storage, and transfer facilities.
- Reduce selected surface parking.
- Begin routing shared autonomous services around Volkswagen shift demand.

#### Phase 3: Network Reshaping

- Redesign traffic circulation.
- Discourage through-traffic in selected areas.
- Prioritize shared mobility, bikes, and pedestrians.
- Expand hub coverage.
- Reclaim larger parking lots.

#### Phase 4: Citywide Post-Car Centre

- Large-scale reduction of private parking.
- Full integration of AV fleet and MaaS.
- Street conversion into public space.
- Strong connection between hubs, urban programs, and public life.

### 12.5 Reclaimed Land Strategy

The project must define what freed car land becomes.

Categories:

- Climate infrastructure.
- Public space.
- Urban gardens.
- New housing.
- Local commerce.
- Mobility plazas.
- Logistics micro-depots.
- Sports and play.
- Community ownership or stewardship.
- Biodiversity corridors.
- Event spaces.
- Productive landscapes.

Avoid making every reclaimed street look the same. Different urban conditions should produce different futures.

## 14. Learning From Proven Traffic Transformations Without Shifting the Project Away From AVs

The project can learn from existing city transformations without becoming a conventional cycling or traffic-calming project.

The key lesson is:

> Mobility behavior changes when policy, design, and infrastructure make alternatives more direct, reliable, and convenient than private driving.

The project does not need to directly structure itself around any one precedent. Instead, precedents can support specific arguments:

- Network design matters.
- Traffic circulation can reduce unnecessary car use.
- Removing parking does not automatically destroy city life.
- Streets can become public spaces, not only traffic corridors.
- Policy and design must work together.
- Initial resistance does not mean long-term failure.
- Accessibility can be maintained without car dominance.

For this project, the precedent lesson should be used to strengthen the AV/MaaS vision:

> The future shared autonomous system will only work if supported by policy, street design, network hierarchy, and hub infrastructure.

## 16. What Makes This Project Different From a Normal Smart Mobility Proposal

Many smart mobility projects focus on technology:

- Apps.
- Sensors.
- Autonomous vehicles.
- Optimization.
- Electrification.
- Digital platforms.

This project should avoid becoming only a technology proposal.

Its originality is that it connects technology to urban form.

The key questions are spatial:

- What land is released when private cars are no longer stored everywhere?
- What replaces parking as the organizing infrastructure of the city?
- How do hubs reshape everyday mobility?
- How does the city feel when traffic space becomes public space?
- What new programs emerge from reclaimed car land?
- How does the Volkswagen commuter system become a catalyst for wider transformation?

The project is therefore an urban design project about mobility, not a mobility project with urban renderings attached.

## 20. Tone and Positioning

The project should be ambitious but not naive.

It should not say:

> Autonomous vehicles will magically solve car dependency.

Instead, it should say:

> Autonomous vehicles make a different ownership model possible, but only if combined with MaaS, hubs, network redesign, policy, digital interfaces, and urban design.

The project’s credibility comes from showing that the future system has a spatial logic, not only a technological fantasy.

## 21. Core Statements to Reuse

> The project is not about replacing every private car with an autonomous car. It is about replacing the urban logic of private vehicle storage with a shared system of access.

> Parking is the infrastructure of private ownership. Hubs are the infrastructure of shared mobility.

> The autonomous vehicle is not the city-making element by itself. The mobility hub is the spatial anchor that allows autonomous mobility to become urban transformation.

> Wolfsburg is a strong prototype because its commuter demand is unusually predictable. The Volkswagen shift system turns mobility demand into something that can be planned, scheduled, and shared.

> The goal is not only to move people more efficiently, but to give the city back to people.

> A post-car city is not a city without movement. It is a city where movement no longer requires storing private vehicles everywhere.

> MaaS becomes plausible when autonomous mobility removes the individual’s responsibility for operating, maintaining, and managing the vehicle.

> The hub is the station of the post-car city.

> Traditional mobility planning asks where cars go. This project asks where access happens.
