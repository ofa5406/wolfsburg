# 05 — Precedent Case-Study Library
### Wolfsburg Post-Car City | Mobility Hubs as the New Organizing Element

This is the working evidence base for the studio (June 25) and the Wolfsburg Award for Urban Vision (Aug 16). Each entry gives **what they did → the hard outcome numbers → what transfers to Wolfsburg**. Figures are cited inline; where the literature disagrees, both numbers are shown and flagged as *contested*.

> **How to read the relevance to our scheme:** Our thesis is a 68-hub, three-tier network sitting on a 5-zone filtered-permeability structure with one-way internal streets and restricted zone entrances. The precedents below are sorted to mirror that logic: first the **sector / circulation-plan** family (the backbone of our 5-zone idea), then **car-reduction & reclamation** cases, then the **evidence base** (traffic evaporation) and the **car-park reuse** analogue for our six L-hubs.

---

## A. The sector / filtered-permeability family (backbone of our 5-zone structure)

### 1. Groningen — Verkeerscirculatieplan (1977) ★ direct inspiration

**What they did.** On the night of 19 September 1977 Groningen split its city centre into **four sectors**. Cars could no longer cross from one sector to another; to reach an adjacent sector a driver had to return to the outer ring road and re-enter. Bicycles and pedestrians kept full permeability through new continuous cycle routes. This is the textbook origin of **filtered permeability** applied at city scale.

**Key outcomes.**
- Inner-city **car traffic roughly halved** after implementation. ([Bicycle Dutch](https://bicycledutch.wordpress.com/2016/03/08/groningen-cycling-city-of-the-netherlands/))
- Today **~60% of all trips in the city are by bicycle** — among the highest of any city its size. ([Bicycle Dutch](https://bicycledutch.wordpress.com/2016/03/08/groningen-cycling-city-of-the-netherlands/))
- Revitalised, commercially stronger city centre; the plan is credited as the template later copied across the Netherlands and beyond. ([inkl / The Guardian republish](https://www.inkl.com/news/how-groningen-invented-a-cycling-template-for-cities-all-over-the-world))
- Implementation was **literally overnight**, with political controversy but durable results (academic review: [WIT Press / van den Berg](https://www.witpress.com/Secure/elibrary/papers/UT04/UT04029FU.pdf)).

**Transfer to Wolfsburg.** This *is* our model. Our 5 zones = Groningen's 4 sectors scaled up. Key lessons to cite on June 25: (a) cars are not banned, only *re-routed* — every destination stays reachable, just not as a through-route; (b) the cycling network must stay **fully permeable and continuous** exactly where the car network is cut; (c) a decisive "switch-on" moment is politically risky but avoids years of half-measures. Use Groningen to defend the *legitimacy* of one-way internal streets + restricted zone entrances.

### 2. Ghent — Circulatieplan (2017) ★ closest modern analogue

**What they did.** A modern re-run of Groningen at larger scale. Ghent divided its centre into **six sectors** around a car-free core; through-traffic between sectors must use the R40 ring road. Implemented over **one weekend** for **~€5 million**: ~80 streets reconfigured, 2,500 signs changed, 14 points closed to motor traffic.

**Key outcomes.**
- **Cycling in the centre +50%** (2016→2018). ([Stad Gent](https://stad.gent/en/mobility-ghent/circulation-plan))
- Car traffic to/from the centre **−17%**; public transport **+6%**. ([Stad Gent](https://stad.gent/en/mobility-ghent/circulation-plan))
- **35% cycling modal share** — a 2030 target hit in **2019, eleven years early**. ([Greater Auckland review](https://www.greateraucland.org.nz/2020/01/21/modeshift-targets-critical-lessons-from-ghent/), [TransportXtra](https://www.transportxtra.com/publications/parking-review/news/73807/ghent-a-city-plan-that-transformed-transport-and-livability/))
- Car-free area expanded **128% overnight**; tourism and street life grew rather than declined. ([EU Tourism Platform](https://transition-pathways.europa.eu/tourism/articles/ghent-quiet-streets-vibrant-tourism))

**Transfer to Wolfsburg.** Ghent is the precedent that proves the Groningen model still works in the 2010s–2020s, on a contemporary mid-sized city, **cheaply and fast**. Cite it as the budget/feasibility argument: a sector plan is mostly signage, filters and ring-road management — not megastructure. Six Ghent sectors ≈ our five zones; their ring = our restricted-entrance perimeter.

### 3. Houten — cycling town with car ring (built form analogue)

**What they did.** A new town designed from the ground up so that the **car network is a ring** feeding internal sub-areas, while the **bicycle network is the direct, fine-grained grid** through the centre. The road network is split into **five segments** by permeable barriers (bollards/gates that pass bikes and pedestrians but not cars); cars must use the ring to go between segments. Bike routes branch from a "trunk" like a tree.

**Key outcomes.**
- For trips under 7.5 km, **~42% by bike and ~21% on foot**. ([Cycling Weekly](https://www.cyclingweekly.com/news/a-town-built-for-bikes-32377))
- Residents measurably **more physically active** than neighbouring towns and the Dutch average. ([Cycling Weekly](https://www.cyclingweekly.com/news/a-town-built-for-bikes-32377))
- Named **Cycling City of the Netherlands 2018**. ([Bicycle Dutch](https://bicycledutch.wordpress.com/2018/01/16/houten-cycling-city-of-the-netherlands-2018/))

**Transfer to Wolfsburg.** Houten gives us the **diagram**: ring for cars, permeable mesh for bikes, internal segments entered only from the ring. It is almost identical to our zone-entrance + one-way internal logic, but as *designed urban form* rather than retrofit — useful to show the jury that our structure is a recognised, award-winning typology, not an experiment. Its **five segments** are a clean visual parallel to our five zones.

## B. Car-reduction, reclamation & superblock cases

### 4. Barcelona — Superilles / Superblocks

**What they did.** Groups of ~3×3 city blocks (~400×400 m) where through-traffic is removed from interior streets, internal speeds drop to ~10 km/h, and the freed roadway becomes plazas, play and planting — the car-land-to-public-life move at the heart of our thesis.

**Key outcomes (health modelling — flag as *modelled, not measured*).**
- Full 503-superblock build-out could **prevent ~667 premature deaths/year**. ([ISGlobal](https://www.isglobal.org/en/-/el-proyecto-original-de-las-supermanzanas-podria-evitar-cerca-de-700-muertes-prematuras-anuales-en-barcelona))
- Breakdown: **air pollution 291**, **noise 163**, **heat 117** deaths/year avoided. ([ISGlobal](https://www.isglobal.org/en/-/el-proyecto-original-de-las-supermanzanas-podria-evitar-cerca-de-700-muertes-prematuras-anuales-en-barcelona))
- Estimated **−24% annual air pollution (NO₂)** city-wide if fully built. ([ISGlobal](https://www.isglobal.org/en/-/el-proyecto-original-de-las-supermanzanas-podria-evitar-cerca-de-700-muertes-prematuras-anuales-en-barcelona))
- *Contested:* these are health-impact-assessment projections for the **full** plan, which has only been partially realised — present as potential, not delivered.

**Transfer to Wolfsburg.** This is our quantitative **health/quality-of-life argument** for converting freed car-land to public space. The ISGlobal numbers let us say "reclaiming street space is a measurable public-health intervention," with the honest caveat that they are modelled.

### 5. Paris — Hidalgo, 15-minute city & parking removal

**What they did.** A decade of lane removal, protected cycle network ("Plan Vélo"), pedestrianised riverbanks, and a programme to remove on-street parking — framed politically as the **15-minute city**.

**Key outcomes.**
- Plan to remove **~70,000 on-street parking spaces** (cited as up to **72%** of on-street supply; ~60,000 of 83,500 in another count — *figures vary by source/date*). ([ITS International](https://www.itsinternational.com/its3/news/paris-get-rid-70000-parking-spaces), [Park4SUMP](https://park4sump.eu/news-events/news/why-paris-eliminating-72-its-street-parking-spaces))
- **Bike modal share roughly doubled, ~5% (2020) → >11% (2025)**. ([Urban Cycling Institute](https://urbancyclinginstitute.substack.com/p/reclaiming-the-city-pariss-decade))
- Cars are **~13% of trips yet occupy ~50% of street space** — the core spatial-injustice statistic. ([Park4SUMP](https://park4sump.eu/news-events/news/why-paris-eliminating-72-its-street-parking-spaces))

**Transfer to Wolfsburg.** Paris is our headline **parking-to-people reallocation** precedent and the political brand for **proximity / 15-minute** living. The "13% of trips, 50% of space" line is a strong opening slide. Freed parking → cycle lanes, gardens, play — exactly our freed-car-land programme.

### 6. Pontevedra — near-total car-free centre

**What they did.** From 1999 the mayor pedestrianised the entire historic centre (~300,000 m²), closed surface car parks and moved parking to free underground/edge lots, replaced lights with roundabouts, and imposed 30/10 km/h limits.

**Key outcomes.**
- **−97% traffic in the historic centre**; ~**90%** of through-car-traffic removed city-wide over ~20 years. ([CityChangers](https://citychangers.org/pontevedra-the-little-utopian-city-that-drove-out-cars/))
- **CO₂ down ~61–70%** (*range reflects different baselines/methods*). ([CityChangers](https://citychangers.org/pontevedra-the-little-utopian-city-that-drove-out-cars/))
- **Zero traffic deaths since 2011**; city centre **gained population (~12,000 new residents)**. ([CityChangers](https://citychangers.org/pontevedra-the-little-utopian-city-that-drove-out-cars/))
- ~70% of trips now on foot or by bike. ([CityChangers](https://citychangers.org/pontevedra-the-little-utopian-city-that-drove-out-cars/))

**Transfer to Wolfsburg.** Pontevedra is the **"edge/underground parking + free centre" operating model** — almost exactly our hub-at-the-edge, free-interior concept. Cite it for the safety and **city-centre repopulation** argument: removing cars *grew* the resident population rather than killing the centre.

### 7. Oslo — car-free / parking-removal in the centre (2019)

**What they did.** Rather than a hard ban, Oslo removed almost all on-street car parking inside the inner ring (replaced with cycle lanes, benches, parklets) plus targeted street closures — a "remove the parking, not the car" strategy.

**Key outcomes.**
- **~700–800 on-street parking spaces removed** by 2019. ([CityChangers](https://citychangers.org/how-oslo-hit-reverse-on-its-city-street-parking/))
- Centre car traffic **−11% (2016–18)** then **−19% (2018–19)**; inner-city areas **−28%** overall. ([EU Urban Mobility Observatory](https://urban-mobility-observatory.transport.ec.europa.eu/resources/case-studies/oslo-promoting-active-transport-modes_en))
- **Zero pedestrian and cyclist fatalities** in the centre in 2019. ([Medium / Vision Zero Cities](https://medium.com/vision-zero-cities-journal/how-oslo-reached-vision-zero-b952aed44697))
- **No significant retail revenue loss**; opposition softened over time. ([CityChangers](https://citychangers.org/how-oslo-hit-reverse-on-its-city-street-parking/))

**Transfer to Wolfsburg.** Oslo is the precedent for our **specific mechanism — attack the parking, not the driver**. It directly rebuts the "businesses will die" objection (the retail-revenue finding) and supports phasing parking removal alongside hub roll-out.

### 8. Vienna — Supergrätzl (Favoriten) & mobility

**What they did.** Vienna's first "superblock" pilot (Favoriten, 2022) used tactical-urbanism filters, one-way loops and "modal filters" to stop through-traffic across a cluster of streets, then made it permanent (opened May 2026).

**Key outcomes.**
- Pilot delivered **60 new trees, 94 added green spaces**, new pedestrian zones, seating and community gardens around Herzgasse school. ([Studio LAUT](https://laut.studio/supergraetzl-favoriten/))
- Through-traffic prevented via one-way loops + modal filters; **temporary measures made permanent**. ([Stadt Wien](https://www.wien.gv.at/stadtplanung/supergraetzl-favoriten))

**Transfer to Wolfsburg.** Vienna shows the **tactical-to-permanent pathway** and the **one-way-loop + modal-filter toolkit** we propose for internal streets — useful for the *phasing* narrative (test cheaply, then fix in concrete) and for a German-speaking-context precedent the jury will recognise.

## C. The mechanism evidence: traffic evaporation

### 9. "Disappearing traffic" — Cairns, Atkins & Goodwin

**What it is.** The foundational meta-study answering the universal objection: *"if you take road space away, won't the cars just clog the rest?"* Reviewed **70+ cases of road-space reallocation across 11 countries**, plus 200+ professionals' input.

**Key findings.**
- When road capacity is cut, a meaningful share of traffic **does not reappear elsewhere — it evaporates** (trips suppressed, retimed, combined, or shifted modes). ([Cairns, Atkins & Goodwin PDF, hosted by NACTO](https://nacto.org/wp-content/uploads/disappearing_traffic_cairns.pdf))
- Excluding outliers, **half of the studied locations saw overall traffic fall by >16%** on affected roads. ([NACTO PDF](https://nacto.org/wp-content/uploads/disappearing_traffic_cairns.pdf))
- Predictions of gridlock were **"often unnecessarily alarmist."** ([NACTO PDF](https://nacto.org/wp-content/uploads/disappearing_traffic_cairns.pdf))

**Transfer to Wolfsburg.** This is our **rebuttal slide**. When the jury or studio critics say "rerouting cars onto the zone perimeter will cause congestion," Cairns/Goodwin is the peer-reviewed answer: a large share of trips disappears rather than displaces. It scientifically backs our one-way internal streets + restricted entrances.

## D. Repurposed multi-storey car park (for our six L-hubs)

### 10. Car-park adaptive reuse — Newcastle-under-Lyme "Karparc" & others

**What they did.** A wave of projects converting redundant multi-storey car parks into housing and mixed use as car demand falls — proving the concrete frames have a second life.

**Key examples.**
- **Karparc, Newcastle-under-Lyme (UK):** approved conversion of the Midway multi-storey into **111 apartments** (one/two/three-bed), gym, cinema, residents' lounge — exposed concrete frame retained. ([Discover SWNS](https://discover.swns.com/2026/03/derelict-multi-storey-car-park-being-transformed-into-111-apartments-in-uk-first/), [Architects' Journal / shedkm](https://www.architectsjournal.co.uk/news/shedkm-wins-approval-to-convert-multistorey-carpark-into-homes))
- **John Lewis (UK):** programme to build **~10,000 homes** on its store car parks over a decade. ([Dezeen](https://www.dezeen.com/2021/07/12/john-lewis-repurpose-car-parks-10000-homes/))
- **SCADpad, Atlanta (US):** micro-apartments built inside a working car park as a proof-of-concept. ([Arcadis insight](https://www.arcadis.com/en/insights/blog/global/arcadis/2023/the-urban-car-park-a-blank-slate-for-architects-and-designers))
- **Melbourne Skyfarm:** rooftop urban farm from disused parking decks. ([Arcadis insight](https://www.arcadis.com/en/insights/blog/global/arcadis/2023/the-urban-car-park-a-blank-slate-for-architects-and-designers))

**Transfer to Wolfsburg.** Directly supports our **six L-hubs that reuse existing multi-storey car parks**. Cite Karparc to show the structural-frame reuse is *consented and built*, not speculative — the floor-to-floor heights and ramps become housing, makerspaces, urban farming or hub functions. It also reinforces the thesis arc: the building type that *symbolised* the parking city becomes the armature of the post-car city.
