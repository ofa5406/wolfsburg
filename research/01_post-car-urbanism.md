# 01 — Post-Car Urbanism: Theory & Foundations
### Wolfsburg Post-Car City | From Parking to Mobility Hubs

This document is the conceptual backbone for the studio (June 25) and competition (Aug 16). It sets out *why* a post-private-car city is defensible, *what* intellectual tradition it sits in, and *how* our central move — replacing **parking** with **mobility hubs** as the organizing element of the neighbourhood — is grounded in theory and evidence. Citations are inline; contested points are flagged.

---

## 1. The post-car / car-reduced city — concept & lineage

The "post-car city" does not mean a city with **no** cars. It means a city where the **private car is no longer the organizing assumption** of urban form — where streets, land allocation, and daily life are designed around walking, cycling, transit and **shared** mobility, with the private car demoted to an occasional, edge-of-system option.

**Intellectual lineage (the chain to cite):**
- **Jane Jacobs (1961), *The Death and Life of Great American Cities*** — attacked the erosion of street life by car-centric planning; argued the street, not the road, is the primary public space and engine of urban vitality. The "eyes on the street" and fine-grained mixed use are the ancestors of today's proximity planning.
- **Colin Buchanan (1963), *Traffic in Towns*** — even this pro-accommodation report admitted there is a ceiling: a city cannot give the car everything it "wants" without destroying environmental quality. Often cited as the moment car-capacity limits entered mainstream planning.
- **Jan Gehl** — decades of work (*Life Between Buildings*, *Cities for People*) reframing design around the human scale and measuring public-life quality; the methodological bridge from critique to practice. (Gehl also writes the foreword to Moreno's 2024 book — see §5.) ([Moreno, *The 15-Minute City*, 2024](https://www.amazon.com/15-Minute-City-Solution-Saving-Planet/dp/1394228147))
- **Donald Shoup (2005)** — exposed **parking** as the hidden subsidy and silent shaper of car-dependent form (see §2).
- **Carlos Moreno (2016)** — packaged proximity into the politically potent **15-minute city** (see §5).
- **Practice codification** — NACTO's *Urban Street Design Guide* and ITDP's *TOD Standard* turned these ideas into measurable street- and network-design rules (see §6).

The through-line: over sixty years the field has moved from *critiquing* car dominance (Jacobs/Buchanan) → *measuring* human-scale alternatives (Gehl) → *diagnosing the mechanism* (Shoup on parking; Cairns/Goodwin on traffic) → *operationalising proximity and reallocation* (Moreno, NACTO, ITDP). **Our project is the next step: making the mobility hub, not the parking space, the unit that organises the neighbourhood.**

## 2. Parking as urban form — Donald Shoup

Shoup's *The High Cost of Free Parking* (2005) is the single most important text for our thesis, because it shows that **parking is not a neutral background condition — it is the invisible code that produces the car-dependent city.**

**The argument.** Cities mandate **minimum parking requirements** (e.g. *N* spaces per dwelling or per 1,000 ft² of retail). These minimums:
- force every building to bundle expensive car storage into its cost, raising rents and suppressing density;
- consume vast land that could be housing or public space;
- subsidise driving and lock in car dependence — a self-fulfilling prophecy.

**The numbers (cite these).**
- Parking is **free for ~99% of car trips in the US** — the "free" parking is paid for by everyone through higher rents and prices. ([Wikipedia: Donald Shoup, summarising Shoup 2005](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Shoup))
- Shoup estimates the **total land + construction value of all US parking exceeds the value of all cars — possibly all roads too.** ([Wikipedia: Donald Shoup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Shoup))
- **~660 ft² (≈61 m²) per parking space** — enough for a second apartment; ~a quarter the floor area of a typical single-family house. ([War on Cars / Shoup interview](https://thewaroncars.org/2023/01/17/the-high-cost-of-free-parking-with-donald-shoup/))
- A structured-parking space costs **~$125/month just to operate and maintain**; surface spaces cost tens of thousands to build. ([War on Cars / Shoup interview](https://thewaroncars.org/2023/01/17/the-high-cost-of-free-parking-with-donald-shoup/))
- Minimum-parking mandates can act as an impact fee **>10× all other development impact fees combined.** ([Wikipedia: Donald Shoup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Shoup))

**Shoup's three reforms:** (1) **remove parking minimums**; (2) **charge the right price** for curb parking (demand-based); (3) **return parking revenue** to the neighbourhood. ([Shoup, "The Trouble with Minimum Parking Requirements"](http://shoup.bol.ucla.edu/Trouble.pdf))

**For our project.** Shoup is the *quantified justification* for converting parking land to public life. Every hub we create is, in Shoupian terms, parking demand **collectivised and priced** rather than mandated and free. The ~61 m²-per-space figure lets us literally calculate the public-space dividend of each parking lot we dissolve into the hub network. *(Caveat: Shoup's figures are US-based; German minimum-parking rules — Stellplatzverordnung at Land level — differ, and several German cities have already relaxed or abolished them. Frame the dollar figures as illustrative of the mechanism, not as German cost data.)*

## 3. Hubs vs. parking — the organizing logic of the neighbourhood

This is the conceptual heart of the project, and it can be stated as a single contrast:

> **Parking is the infrastructure of private ownership. The mobility hub is the infrastructure of sharing.**

| | **Parking (old organizing element)** | **Mobility hub (new organizing element)** |
|---|---|---|
| Logic | Every household stores its *own* idle vehicle near home | A *shared* fleet + transit + active modes pooled at nodes |
| Land use | Dispersed, mandated, idle ~95% of the time | Concentrated, active, multi-functional |
| Spatial effect | Diffuses car storage into every street and plot | Frees the in-between streets for public life |
| Ownership | Private, individual | Collective, access-over-ownership |
| Network role | None — it is a terminus | A **node** in a legible, tiered network |

**What a mobility hub is (definition to cite).** CoMoUK defines mobility hubs as *"highly visible, safe and accessible spaces where public, shared and active travel modes are co-located alongside improvements to public realm and community facilities."* They are interchanges where transit, cycling/walking, and shared transport (car clubs, bike-share, e-scooters) meet — and they **redesign space to reduce private-car space and improve public realm.** ([CoMoUK](https://www.como.org.uk/news/introducing-the-mobility-hubs-concept)) CoMoUK also defines a **hub typology** (city-centre, corridor, business-park, suburban, rural, tourism) — directly relevant to our **three-tier, 68-hub** classification. ([CoMoUK localplan guidance](https://www.localplan.winchester.gov.uk/assets/inline/1051/ST14-Mobility-hubs-guidance-CoMoUK.pdf))

**Why sharing beats storage — the Bremen evidence.** Bremen's long-running car-sharing programme shows the substitution effect is real and large:
- **Each car-sharing vehicle replaces ~16 private cars** in Bremen. ([Share North](https://share-north.eu/2021/05/mission-accomplished-20000-people-in-bremen-are-using-car-sharing-over-6000-cars-replaced-goals-of-the-carsharing-action-plan-achieved/))
- ~**6,000+ private vehicles removed**; impact valued as equivalent to **~€200 m of avoided parking-garage investment**. ([Bremen best-practice PDF](https://gloricon.de/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/EGUM-best_practice-Bremen-car_sharing.pdf))
- About a third of Bremen's 20,000 car-share users **gave up or never bought a car.** ([Share North](https://share-north.eu/2021/05/mission-accomplished-20000-people-in-bremen-are-using-car-sharing-over-6000-cars-replaced-goals-of-the-carsharing-action-plan-achieved/))

**For our project.** Bremen (a German peer city) is the empirical bridge: if one shared car removes ~16 private ones, then a network of hubs *mathematically* releases the parking land our thesis reclaims. The 16:1 figure is the engine of the whole "parking → public life" conversion.

## 4. Filtered permeability & low-traffic neighbourhoods (the network theory)

This is the theory underneath our **5-zone structure, one-way internal streets, and restricted zone entrances.**

**Filtered permeability** (term coined by **Steve Melia, 2012**): a neighbourhood should be **fully permeable for walking and cycling but deliberately *not* permeable for through motor traffic.** The street grid is kept fine and connected for people on foot and bike; for cars, selected links are filtered so the area can't be used as a cut-through. ([Wikipedia: Permeability / filtered permeability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filtered_permeability), summarising Melia 2012)

**Modal filter** — the physical device that achieves this: a point that passes bikes/pedestrians/sometimes buses but blocks private cars (bollards, planters, bus gates, or — at our scale — restricted zone entrances). ([Wikipedia: Modal filter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modal_filter))

**Low-Traffic Neighbourhood (LTN)** — the applied form. London implemented ~100 LTN schemes by 2022. Evidence (Aldred & Goodman, People and Places survey) associates LTNs with **lower car use and more active travel** for residents inside them, with displacement onto boundary roads far smaller and more temporary than critics predicted. ([Findings Press / Aldred & Goodman](https://findingspress.org/article/17128-low-traffic-neighbourhoods-car-use-and-active-travel-evidence-from-the-people-and-places-survey-of-outer-london-active-travel-interventions))

> **Honest note:** LTNs are politically contested. Studies also document polarised community responses and equity concerns about who benefits ([Birmingham study, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11675817/)). Our boards should acknowledge this and address boundary-road and access concerns directly rather than claim consensus.

**For our project.** Groningen (1977) and Ghent (2017) — see *05_precedents.md* — are filtered permeability at *city-sector* scale; our 5 zones apply the same logic. Melia gives us the **name and the principle**; the modal filter gives us the **device**; the LTN literature gives us **empirical car-reduction evidence** and an honest read on the controversy.

## 5. The 15-minute city / proximity planning — Carlos Moreno

**Carlos Moreno** (Professor, IAE Paris–Sorbonne) introduced the **15-minute city** in **2016**: neighbourhoods where residents reach most daily needs — work, shops, healthcare, education, leisure — within a **15-minute walk or bike ride.** ([Wikipedia: Carlos Moreno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carlos_Moreno_(urbanist)))

**Core concept — *chrono-urbanism***: organising the city around **time and proximity** rather than speed and distance — the pace of life, travel time, and the multi-use of buildings across the day. Moreno frames it through four principles: **proximity, diversity (mixed use), density, and ubiquity (access for all).** ([Moreno's site](https://www.moreno-web.net/the-15-minutes-city-for-a-new-chrono-urbanism-pr-carlos-moreno/))

It is the **demand-side** complement to our supply-side moves: if hubs and filtered permeability *remove* the need to drive, proximity planning ensures the *destinations are already nearby*. Paris (Hidalgo) is the flagship application (see *05_precedents.md*).

> **Honest note:** the 15-minute city has been the target of conspiracy theories framing it as "movement restriction." It is the opposite — *access*, not confinement. Worth a single clear rebuttal line on the boards.

**For our project.** Moreno supplies the **political brand and the human story** ("get your life back / quality of life"), and the four principles map cleanly onto a hub-organised neighbourhood: the hub is where proximity, diversity and ubiquity are made physical.

## 6. Land reclamation & street-space reallocation — "streets as public space"

The final theoretical pillar: once cars no longer organise the ground plane, the freed space is not "leftover" — it is **the city's largest reserve of public land**, already publicly owned.

**The reallocation principle.** Paris crystallised the spatial-justice case: cars are **~13% of trips but occupy ~50% of street space** ([Park4SUMP](https://park4sump.eu/news-events/news/why-paris-eliminating-72-its-street-parking-spaces)). Reallocation simply rebalances space to how people actually move.

**Practice guidance to cite (turns theory into drawn detail):**
- **NACTO, *Urban Street Design Guide*** — codifies the "complete street," the reallocation of carriageway to people, and street-as-public-space design standards (lane widths, parklets, plazas, protected bike lanes). The authoritative design reference for *how* to redraw a reclaimed street.
- **ITDP, *TOD Standard*** and ITDP's parking-reform work — connects density, mixed use and reduced parking to transit-oriented nodes; the network-scale complement to NACTO's street scale.
- **Jan Gehl's methods** — measure public life *before and after* reclamation (pedestrian counts, "staying activities") so the reclaimed space can be evaluated, not just asserted.

**The reclamation arithmetic for Wolfsburg.** Combine Shoup (~61 m²/space) with Bremen (1 shared car ≈ 16 private) and you can *quantify the public-space dividend*: each hub that pools, say, 20 shared vehicles can stand in for ~320 private cars ≈ **~19,500 m² of parking** that converts to streets, gardens and squares. (Use as an illustrative order-of-magnitude, calibrated to our real hub sizing.)

**For our project.** This pillar reframes the freed car-land as our **primary design material**. The hubs concentrate movement; the reclaimed streets between them become the public-life programme that wins the competition.
