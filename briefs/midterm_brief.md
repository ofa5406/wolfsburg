## Purpose of the Midterm

The midterm is not a finished product. By this date the **concept must be locked**, and you should be able to communicate it through a concrete draft of the final outcome that explains itself. The time after midterm is for polishing the communication of your projects.

---

## Format

- **Duration:** 15-minute presentation followed by 15-minute discussion
- **Medium:** Miro frames on the main Studio Board, sequenced for navigation

Each frame is a container for whatever medium best shows the work: video, prototype, walk-through, drawing, or model shot. The frames are not pitch-deck slides full of bullet text.

---

## What to Present

1. **Title** — the project name that captures its essence
2. **Site** — specific location(s) in Wolfsburg: a square, a street, a building, a block. Not "the city."
3. **The artefact / outcome — shown in use.** Whatever form the project takes (prototype, simulation, tool, installation, game, model, design proposal) — walk through a concrete use-case scenario: **who** the user is, **when** and **where** they encounter it, **what** they do with it. Spatial drawings (plan, section, axonometric, 3D, model shot) live inside this point.
4. **Exhibition intent** — a few sentences, and a sketch if available, on how a visitor in the computer pool will encounter this work without you in the room.

> **Reference:** If someone in the 14th century watched the Gutenberg press in action, they did not need a concept pitch to understand what it did or what problem it solved. Your artefact, shown in use, must have the same effect.

---

## What to Avoid

1. **Too many ideas, none deep enough.** Several teams arrived with multiple features or multiple parallel projects.
2. **Abstract vision without concrete site.** "The city" or "public space" is not a site. Pick a real square, street, or building. Problems are only visible at a specific scale.
3. **AI/tech just because** — not a meaningful part of the concept.
4. **Photorealism mistaken for design resolution.** A diagram with a clear idea is more useful than a render of a confused one. Start abstract and refine only where it matters.
5. **App-as-saviour.** Ask: would this still be valuable without the app? If yes, the app is optional. If no, the app needs a reason it doesn't already exist in Google/Apple/Deutsche Bahn.
6. **Slides full of text or generic pitch-deck templates.** Show the work itself: diagrams, prototypes, screenshots, scenes.
7. **Live click-throughs of your website or prototype.** They are slow and fuzzy. Record a 60–90 second screen capture of the moments that matter, add narration, and embed it in your Miro frame. Keep the live demo for the 15-min discussion if someone asks.
8. **Reading out AI-generated text.** Generic LLM output has a recognizable cadence. If you used an LLM to draft your script or captions, rewrite it in your own words before the presentation.
9. **Building the project on a generic problem statement.** "Car dependency", "lack of identity", "problem of communication" — ground your justification in something concrete and observable in Wolfsburg.

## Thinking Ahead to the Exhibition

The **Summaery exhibition** (July 9–12, computer pool) is the public-facing form of the project. Visitors arrive, look at the work, and form an opinion in seconds — without your narration.

Questions to start asking now:
- **What is the visitor's first encounter?** Screen, model, printed object, room, or playable interface?
- **What does it do without you?** If a visitor cannot read the concept from the exhibit alone in under a minute, it is not exhibition-ready.
- **What is the durable artefact?** Slides disappear after Final Presentation. A printed booklet, a model, a recorded walk-through, or a working web app outlives the presentation. Decide what survives the exhibition and design it deliberately.
