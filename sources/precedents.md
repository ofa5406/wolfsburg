# Precedents

Urban design precedents relevant to the Wolfsburg post-car vision.

---

## Groningen, Netherlands — Traffic Zone Model

**Relevance:** Direct inspiration for the 5-zone structure used in the Wolfsburg design.

Groningen divided the city centre into zones where cars cannot cross between zones — they must exit and re-enter via the ring road. This is called **filtered permeability**. The result: through-traffic is eliminated, but residents and businesses retain full access. The city became one of the most cycling-friendly in the world.

**Key lesson for Wolfsburg:** Zone structure changes behaviour without full exclusion. People shift modes when the car route becomes longer than the alternative.

---

## Barcelona Superblocks (Superilles)

**Relevance:** Large-scale reclamation of car space for public life.

Barcelona groups 9 city blocks into a "superblock" where through-traffic is removed from internal streets. The freed street space becomes public — pedestrian zones, play areas, greenery, seating. The exterior streets handle car traffic.

**Key lesson for Wolfsburg:** Car space reclamation produces real urban life gains, not just "nicer corridors." The scale of reclaimed space becomes the project's main argument.

---

## Paris — Voies Lyonnaises / Plan Vélo

**Relevance:** Rapid network expansion of protected cycling infrastructure.

Paris committed to building a full cycling network across the city within a short timeframe. Protected lanes replaced car lanes and parking. Result: significant increase in cycling mode share even in a traditionally car-oriented city.

**Key lesson for Wolfsburg:** Political commitment + physical infrastructure changes behaviour fast. The cycling component of the Wolfsburg system is credible if supported by a real network.

---

## Amsterdam / Netherlands Generally

**Relevance:** Long-term vision of mobility as a right to access, not vehicle movement.

Dutch planning shifted in the 1970s from car-priority to prioritizing pedestrians, cyclists, and public transport. Now used as the baseline for "what a post-car city looks like in practice."

**Key lesson for Wolfsburg:** The post-car city is not a fantasy. It already exists at scale.

---

## Copenhagen — Mobility Hub Programme

**Relevance:** Direct precedent for the hub typology system — Copenhagen developed a city-wide mobility hub programme defining three hub tiers, element palettes, and design standards for multimodal interchange points across the city.

**Key lesson for Wolfsburg:** Hubs work as a legible system when they share a consistent design language. Copenhagen's programme demonstrates how modular elements can be assembled context-specifically while remaining recognizable as a network.

*Search: "Copenhagen mobility hubs guide" — City of Copenhagen, Technical and Environmental Administration. Also documented via State of Green (stateofgreen.com).*

---

## Barcelona Superblock Street Furniture

**Relevance:** Barcelona's Superblock interventions produced a family of context-sensitive street furniture elements — game tables, seating, planting, flexible surfaces — placed in reclaimed car space. These define the social and cultural layer that a hub network can carry.

**Key lesson for Wolfsburg:** Reclaimed car space gains social credibility through quality, permanent-feeling design — not temporary furniture. The Barcelona pieces (chess tables, ping pong, generous seating) made public life in the street feel normal and wanted.

*Search: "Barcelona Superblock furniture" / "Superilles Barcelona urban furniture" — Barcelona City Council, ajuntament.barcelona.cat*

---

## Vienna Supergrätzl

**Relevance:** Vienna's Supergrätzl pilot reclaimed car-dominated blocks using temporary and permanent interventions — pop-up parks, mobile furniture, bioswales, and play elements. The process demonstrated community acceptance and the spatial potential of reclaimed streets.

**Key lesson for Wolfsburg:** The transition from car space to public life can be staged — temporary installations build acceptance, permanent design follows. Relevant for Wolfsburg's 4-phase implementation strategy.

*Search: "Wien Supergrätzl" — City of Vienna, wien.gv.at/verkehr*

---

## Zurich — Enclosed Tram Shelters (VBZ)

**Relevance:** Zürich's tram and bus shelters are designed as proper enclosed, heated waiting spaces — significantly more than a canopy. They demonstrate how public transport infrastructure can be designed with high spatial and material quality, creating genuine comfort rather than minimal coverage.

**Key lesson for Wolfsburg:** The PT shed / canopy element in the hub system should be understood as a quality public space element, not standard transit furniture. Zurich's shelters are a benchmark for what the canopy can aspire to.

*Search: "VBZ Zürich Wartehäuschen" / "ZVV Haltestellen Design" — Verkehrsbetriebe Zürich, vbz.ch*
